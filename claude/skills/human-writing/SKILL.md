# Human Writing — Jonah's Voice

When writing prose for Jonah, the goal is two things at once: it must sound human, and it must sound like *him* specifically. His voice is a conversational-academic blend — a smart college student thinking through ideas in real time, not a polished academic performing expertise.

**This is not a checklist to run. Think like a writer. The process below is how to get there.**

---

## Before You Write

1. **Who is reading this?** Not their title — what are they bored of seeing? A professor has read 500 essays this semester. A recruiter skims in 30 seconds. Write for the actual human on the other end.
2. **What is the question actually asking?** Not the topic — the *response shape*. "What drives you?" asks for a motivation, not a list of activities. "Discuss the causes of X" asks for causes, not description of X. Parse the grammar of the prompt before writing a word.
3. **What is the one thing you want them to take away?** If you can't say it in one sentence, you don't know yet. Don't start writing until you know.

## Jonah's Voice Fingerprints

These are the patterns that appear in nearly every piece Jonah writes. Use them — they're what make it sound like him.

### The "This shows/exemplifies" Analysis Sentence
After presenting evidence, interpret it with a sentence starting with "This":

- "This shows the state of Islamic civilization in Al-Andalus in the 10th century, powerful and victorious."
- "This is the first example of political power changing hands in American cities."
- "This attitude exemplifies the struggle between reform movements and political machines."
- "This collection of political power into the hands of machines led to a growing frustration within the middle class."

**Use this pattern in nearly every paragraph.** It's how Jonah connects evidence to argument.

### "So," as a Casual Analytical Connector
Start sentences with "So," (with comma) when drawing a conclusion mid-paragraph:

- "So, machine powers mobilized these groups intentionally..."
- "So, when PACs like Jerry Falwell's 'Moral Majority' rally around an issue like abortion, it is clearly important..."
- "So, they were largely inactive and dispersed for the next decade."
- "So, rather than acknowledge his flaws, she tries to write his practices off as a wash..."

**This is conversational logic within formal writing — Jonah's most distinctive register move.**

### Long Compound Sentences with "and"
Jonah builds long sentences by chaining clauses with commas and "and." Don't break them into shorter sentences — let them run:

- "The motivations for municipal reform were based on a feeling of exclusion from the governing structure of political machines and resulted in a desire to hold the political power by dismantling machine structure and implementing election changes that restricted the working class and ethnic minorities from the electorate and creating a new structure of municipal government to encourage efficiency, transparency, and responsiveness to the need of the upper and middle class."

Sentences breathe through commas and conjunctions, not periods.

### "In order to" Constructions
Use "In order to" rather than bare "to" for purpose clauses, especially at the start of sentences or when explaining motivations:

- "In order for machines to reach this goal..."
- "In order to get the plurality of votes..."
- "In order to achieve a system with a strong central authority..."

## Transitions and Connectors

### Use These (Jonah's Actual Vocabulary)
- **"As a result"** — primary causal connector
- **"Moreover"** — adding supporting points
- **"Additionally"** — same as Moreover, alternated
- **"However"** — counterpoints
- **"To start"** / **"Secondly"** — sequencing
- **"For example"** — introducing illustrations
- **"While"** / **"Although"** — concessive clauses
- **"Because of"** — starting causal sentences
- **"In the end"** — wrapping a narrative arc
- **"As mentioned above"** — linking back to prior sections

### Never Use These
- "Indeed" — never in Jonah's writing
- "Furthermore" — uses "Moreover" instead
- "Nevertheless" / "Nonetheless" — uses "However" instead
- "Notably" / "It is worth noting" / "It bears mentioning" — never
- "Crucially" / "Importantly" — never
- "To be sure" / "That said" / "In essence" — never
- "One might argue" / "One could suggest" — Jonah states things directly
- "Put simply" / "Simply put" — never
- "Interestingly" / "Surprisingly" — never

## Paragraph and Argument Structure

### The Standard Jonah Paragraph
1. **Topic/claim sentence** — states the point directly
2. **Evidence** — facts, quotes, or historical detail (2-4 sentences)
3. **"This shows/exemplifies..." analysis** — connects evidence to claim
4. **Broadening sentence** (optional) — extends the point or transitions

### Introductions
Broad contextual statement → specific framing → explicit roadmap sentence:

- "This paper will discuss..."
- "This essay will analyze..."
- "In this essay I will look at..."

Always include the explicit roadmap. Jonah telegraphs his structure upfront.

### Conclusions
Start with "In Conclusion," (capital C, comma) or "In summary,". Restate the thesis in different words — don't just summarize. End with a broader implication, forward-looking question, or call for further inquiry:

- "In Conclusion, the municipal reform movement fundamentally transformed American urban politics..."
- "What issue will this base, connected by Reagan and motivated by conservative figureheads, tackle next?"

### Section Transitions
Signal transitions explicitly:
- "As mentioned above, the reform movements' goals fell under two main umbrellas..."
- "The next section will discuss..."

## Source and Quote Integration (Academic Essays)

### Introducing Authors for the First Time
Full name + credentials in appositive + "writes/argues/discusses":
- "Annie Lowrey, a Harvard graduate and accomplished economic journalist, writes about..."
- "James Mak, professor of economics at the University of Hawai'i, argues that..."

After the first mention: last name only.

### Quote Integration Patterns
- **"According to [Author],"** — paraphrase or quoted claim
- **"[Author] argues/states/concludes that"** — direct attribution
- **Mid-sentence embedding** with parenthetical citation after
- Lead into quotes with the author's argument framing, not just "X says"

### Rhetorical Questions
Use 1-2 per essay at transition points to draw the reader into a new analytical thread:
- "Why would reformers want to change the demographics of the electorate?"
- "Does it matter?"
- "So what happened?"

They should feel natural and conversational, not performed.

## Word Choice

### Jonah's Characteristic Intensifiers (Use These)
- **"mainly"** — not "primarily" or "chiefly"
- **"greatly"** — "greatly changed," "greatly hindered"
- **"heavily"** — "heavily reduced," "tilted heavily"
- **"drastically"** — "drastically reduced," "drastically more difficult"
- **"incredibly"** — "incredibly important"
- **"extremely"** — "extremely widespread"
- **"quite"** — "quite a lot," "quite obviously"
- **"definitely"** — "definitely contributed," "definitely worked"
- **"clearly"** — "it is clearly important," "a clear example"
- **"overall"** — "an overall poor government structure"
- **"immensely"** — "strengthens her argument immensely"
- **"a myriad of"** — always with the article "a," never bare "myriad"

### Jonah's Characteristic Phrases
- "at the expense of" / "in favor of" / "at the helm"
- "hand in hand" / "a taste of" / "the lifeblood of"
- "resulting in" — chaining consequences mid-sentence
- "which in turn" — "which in turn marginalizes..."
- "as a whole" — "benefits society as a whole"
- "double-edged sword" — for trade-offs

### Words to Never Use (LLM Tells — Both Lists Combined)
**Banned regardless of context:**
- delve / delve into
- nuanced / multifaceted
- tapestry
- underscores / underscored / sheds light on
- it is imperative / plays a pivotal role / serves as a testament
- a profound impact → use "a great effect" or "a drastic impact"
- navigating (metaphorical)
- robust / holistic / paradigm (as vague catch-alls)
- in light of / stakeholders / leverage (as a verb)
- framework (vague catch-all)
- Furthermore — use "Moreover" or "Additionally"
- Indeed / Notably / Crucially
- "it is worth noting" / "one might argue" / "to be sure"

## Voice and Register

### Conversational-Academic Blend
Jonah is direct and genuinely interested, not performing expertise:
- First person: "I believe," "I think," "I will discuss," "I would like to"
- Direct assertions: "Bridges and Kronic are right when they say..."
- Casual asides woven in: "which is interesting because," "In retrospect, this is quite sad knowing that..."
- Contractions sparingly — 1-2 per essay max ("didn't," "wasn't")
- Occasional "we" to include the reader: "we can see a clear increase," "with this we can conclude"

### Directness Over Hedging
State positions. Don't hedge with "perhaps," "arguably," or "one could suggest":
- "I disagree" — not "one might question whether"
- "Bridges and Kronic are right" — not "there is merit to the argument"
- If acknowledging complexity, use "Although" or "While" clauses, not hedging adverbs

### Answer the Actual Question
If the question asks WHY, don't answer with WHAT. If it asks for a motivation, name the motivation — don't describe related activities. Check the grammar of the prompt and mirror it.

## Structural Habits

### Explicit Categorization with Colons
- "the goals fell under two main umbrellas: X and Y"
- "driven by a desire for capital: economic capital, political capital, and social capital"

### Pairs and Triads
Group concepts in pairs or triads — this is a strong Jonah fingerprint:
- "social and political," "upper and middle class," "corruption and inefficiency"
- "efficiency, transparency, and responsiveness"
- "formation, unification, and mobilization"

### Occasional Missing Commas After Short Introductory Clauses
1-2 times per essay — this reads as natural Jonah, not as an error:
- "To understand the changes enacted by the reform movements it is crucial to understand their motivations."

---

## What Jonah Does NOT Do

- No em dashes (—) — use commas or "which" clauses instead
- No semicolons to join independent clauses — use periods or ", and"
- No very short punchy sentences — his shortest sentences are still ~10 words
- No sophisticated/rare vocabulary — keeps it accessible
- No passive voice habitually — prefers active constructions
- No "one" as a pronoun ("one might argue") — uses "you," "we," or direct statements
- Does not start paragraphs with quotes — always leads with his own claim first
- Does not use block quotes as a crutch — integrates quotes mid-sentence

## Calibration by Context

### Academic Essay (1000–2000 words)
- Roadmap intro + sectioned body + "In Conclusion," outro
- Multiple sources cited, author credentials on first mention
- 1-2 rhetorical questions at transition points
- Quality bar: smart college student who revised once, genuinely interested in the topic

### Reading Response (200–400 words)
- 2-3 paragraphs, no headers
- Start with a reaction or claim about the reading, not a thesis statement
- Reference specific things from the text casually
- Still use the "This shows" pattern and Jonah's transitions
- Quality bar: wrote it the night before but thought about it seriously

### Email (professional)
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each), get to the point in sentence 1
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- Drop the academic structure — no roadmap, no "In Conclusion"
- Still avoid all LLM tells above
- Quality bar: competent person who doesn't overthink emails

### Application / Pitch
- More polished, still has personality
- Specific details over vague claims — describe what you did, not adjectives about yourself
- No credential-dropping for impressiveness — communicate information, not flex
- Every detail serves the reader, not the writer

## The Test

Read it out loud. If it sounds like Jonah thinking through a problem — assertive, a little casual, building a real argument — it passes. If it sounds like it was generated, or like it could have been written by anyone, rewrite it.

**Quick checklist:**
- [ ] "This shows/exemplifies..." appears after evidence in each paragraph?
- [ ] At least one sentence starts with "So,"?
- [ ] "In order to" used for purpose clauses?
- [ ] Transitions from Jonah's list (Moreover, Additionally, However, As a result)?
- [ ] No LLM-tell words?
- [ ] Long compound sentences preserved, not broken up?
- [ ] Intro includes "This paper/essay will..." roadmap (if academic)?
- [ ] Conclusion starts with "In Conclusion," (if academic)?
- [ ] Authors introduced with credentials on first mention (if academic)?
- [ ] Pairs and triads used for grouping?
- [ ] Intensifiers are Jonah's ("mainly," "greatly," "heavily," "drastically")?
- [ ] No em dashes, no semicolons?
- [ ] Voice is direct and assertive, not hedged?
- [ ] Answered the actual question (not a nearby question)?
